Economic Geology

Economic geology is the branch of geology that deals with earth materials of economic value, focusing on the origin, discovery, extraction, and use of mineral and energy resources.

Terms in Economic Geology

Term Definition
Ore Rock or material that contains sufficient quantities of valuable minerals or metals that can be extracted profitably.
Deposit A naturally occurring concentration of minerals or hydrocarbons that is economically viable to extract.
Porphyry A large, disseminated mineral deposit formed from hydrothermal fluids associated with a cooling intrusive body, typically rich in copper, molybdenum, and sometimes gold.
Vein A sheet-like body of crystallized minerals within a rock, often formed from hydrothermal fluids and frequently hosting ore minerals.
Ore mineral A mineral that contains a metal or valuable element that can be economically extracted (e.g., chalcopyrite for copper, galena for lead).
Gangue mineral The non-valuable minerals found within an ore deposit that are discarded during ore processing (e.g., quartz, feldspar).
Reservoir A porous and permeable geological formation that stores and transmits oil or gas; usually overlain by an impermeable cap rock that traps the hydrocarbons.
Source rock A sedimentary rock rich in organic material that, under heat and pressure, generates and releases oil or gas into adjacent reservoir rocks.
Trap A geological structure that impedes the upward movement of hydrocarbons, allowing them to accumulate in a reservoir; examples include anticlines, faults, and salt domes.
Cap rock An impermeable rock layer, such as shale or salt, that overlies a reservoir rock and prevents the escape of hydrocarbons.
Migration The movement of hydrocarbons from a source rock into a reservoir rock through porous rocks or fractures.

Formation of Mineral Resources

Source: https://www.geologyin.com

  • Mineral deposits form through several geological processes that concentrate elements into economically valuable quantities.
    • Magmatic Processes
    • Hydrothermal Processes
    • Sedimentary Processes
    • Metamorphic Processes
    • Supergene Enrichment

Magmatic Processes

  • Definition: Crystallization of minerals from cooling magma.
  • Mechanism: Dense minerals settle out early from mafic magmas.
  • Common Deposits:
    • Chromite
    • Magnetite
    • Platinum-group elements (PGEs)

Hydrothermal Processes

  • Definition: Deposition of minerals from hot, metal-rich fluids.
  • Mechanism: Fluids move through fractures, depositing minerals as temperature or chemistry changes.
  • Examples:
    • Porphyry copper deposits
    • Gold-quartz veins
    • Lead-zinc veins

Sedimentary Processes

  • Definition: Metal concentration via chemical or physical sedimentation.
  • Mechanisms:
    • Chemical precipitation in marine basins
    • Mechanical concentration in rivers
  • Examples:
    • Banded Iron Formations (BIFs)
    • Placer deposits (e.g., gold, tin, diamonds)

Metamorphic Processes

  • Definition: Re-distribution of metals during metamorphism.
  • Mechanism: Heat and pressure mobilize fluids that enrich certain zones.
  • Examples:
    • Shear zone-hosted gold
    • Graphite deposits

Supergene Enrichment

  • Definition: Weathering near the surface leads to metal enrichment at depth.
  • Mechanism: Leaching removes metals from the upper zone and reprecipitates them below.
  • Examples:
    • Enriched copper zones below oxide caps
    • Silver enrichment in epithermal systems

Metallic Minerals

Metallic minerals are naturally occurring substances that contain metal elements in sufficient quantities to make extraction economically viable. These minerals are typically found in igneous, metamorphic, or sedimentary rocks, and are mined for the metals they contain.

Key Characteristics of Metallic Minerals

  • Have metallic luster (they look shiny or reflective).
  • Are generally dense and conduct electricity and heat well.
  • Can be melted to extract the metal.
  • Often occur in veins, lodes, or disseminated deposits.

Examples of Metallic Minerals

Mineral Metal Extracted
Chalcopyrite Copper (Cu)
Galena Lead (Pb)
Hematite Iron (Fe)
Bauxite Aluminum (Al)
Sphalerite Zinc (Zn)
Wolframite Tungsten (W)
Ilmenite Titanium (Ti)
Gold/Silver Precious metals (Au, Ag)

Non-Metallic Minerals

Non-metallic minerals are naturally occurring minerals that do not contain metals or do not yield metallic products when processed. These minerals are used in their natural form or after minimal processing, often in construction, agriculture, and industrial applications.

Key Characteristics of Non-Metallic Minerals

  • Do not have metallic luster (often dull or earthy).
  • Are usually insulators (do not conduct heat or electricity).
  • Do not produce metals when melted or processed.
  • Are typically found in sedimentary or metamorphic rocks.

Examples of Non-Metallic Minerals

Mineral Use/Application

Gypsum Plaster, drywall, fertilizer
Phosphate Fertilizers
Barite Drilling mud in oil and gas industry
Anhydrite Cement, soil treatment
Sulfur Sulfuric acid production, fertilizers
Quartz Glass, electronics, construction
Clay Ceramics, bricks, paper filler

Building Materials

  • Building materials are naturally occurring rocks and minerals (or products derived from them) that are used in construction and civil engineering projects.
  • They form the foundation of infrastructure, housing, roads, and public works.

Key Characteristics of Building Materials

  • Typically abundant and locally available.
  • Selected for their strength, durability, and resistance to weathering.
  • Can be used in natural form (e.g., stone) or processed (e.g., cement).

Examples of Common Building Materials

Material Use/Application
Sand Concrete, mortar, glass production
Gravel Concrete aggregate, road base
Limestone Cement, construction stone, lime production
Granite Dimensional stone, countertops, cladding
Clay Bricks, tiles, ceramics
Slate Roofing, flooring, wall panels
Gypsum Drywall, plaster, interior finishing

Formation of Oil and Gas

Source: https://energyeducation.ca

The formation of oil and gas involves a series of biological, chemical, and geological processes over millions of years.

1. Organic Matter Accumulation

  • Originates from marine microorganisms like plankton.
  • Their remains settle on the sea floor.
  • In low-oxygen environments, organic matter is preserved in fine-grained muddy sediments.

2. Burial and Source Rock Formation

  • Sediments bury the organic-rich layers.
  • Temperature and pressure increase with depth.
  • Organic-rich shales become source rocks.

3. Thermal Maturation

  • 60â120Â°C: Oil forms in the oil window.
  • >120Â°C: Gas forms as residual matter converts.
  • Heat causes transformation into liquid and gaseous hydrocarbons.

4. Migration

  • Oil and gas migrate upward due to lower density.
  • Move from source rock into porous reservoir rocks like sandstone or limestone.

5. Trapping

  • Movement stops when fluids reach a trap.
  • Cap rocks (e.g., shale, salt) seal the reservoir.
  • Types of traps:
    • Anticline
    • Fault trap
    • Stratigraphic trap
    • Salt dome

6. Reservoir Accumulation

  • Fluids accumulate and segregate by density:
    • Gas on top
    • Oil in the middle
    • Water at the bottom
